The One with the Scatter Plots

Your job is to become the class expert on correlation — how two numerical variables move together. You'll build a correlation matrix that reveals the relationships between many variables at once.

Key Concept

Correlation measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two numerical variables, from -1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive), with 0 meaning no relationship. A correlation matrix lets you see these values across multiple categories at once — so instead of checking one pair at a time, you can spot patterns and outliers across the whole dataset. In Tableau, you build this using the CORR function inside a calculated field, then display the values as colored squares.

This is the key step. You'll use Tableau's CORR function with an INCLUDE Level of Detail expression to calculate the correlation between Sales and Quantity at the order level.

🔍 Think About It
  • Why Sales vs. Quantity? If a sub-category sells more items, you'd expect higher sales — so these should be strongly correlated. But are they always?
  • The INCLUDE expression tells Tableau: "first sum up Sales and Quantity at the Order ID level, then calculate the correlation across those orders."
3

▼
🔍 Think About It
  • Most sub-categories should show strong positive correlations. If one year has a much lower value, what might that mean? (Outlier orders? Unusual discounting?)
  • If you were a manager, which sub-categories would you want to investigate further based on this matrix?
  • What other pairs of measures might be interesting to correlate? (e.g., Discount vs. Profit, Quantity vs. Shipping Cost)

🎤 Preparing Your Teach-Back (~10 min prep)

  2. Walk through the CORR calculated field. Show the class the formula and explain what the INCLUDE expression does — why do we need to roll up to the order level first? (2 min)
  3. Show your matrix. Walk through the color-coded grid. Which sub-categories are strongly correlated? Did any years look different than expected? (2 min)
  4. Show your own exploration. What other measure pairs did you try? What did you find? (2 min)

The One with the Trend Lines

Your job is to become the class expert on regression over time — modeling how sales trend across months and which model type fits best.

Key Concept

Regression models the relationship between variables to identify trends and make predictions. Linear fits a straight line, exponential models growth/decay, and polynomial captures curves. The R² value indicates how well the model fits. A p-value below 0.05 tells you the relationship is statistically significant (probably not random chance).

The One with the Clusters

Your job is to become the class expert on clustering — grouping similar data points together to find natural segments in the data.

Key Concept

Cluster analysis groups data points that share similar characteristics. Tableau uses a k-means algorithm — you choose how many clusters (k) and it assigns each point to its nearest group. This is useful for customer segmentation, identifying outliers, or discovering natural groupings you didn't expect. There's no single "correct" number of clusters — it depends on what's useful for the business question.
