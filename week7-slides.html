Before We Dive In

Tableau Built-In Models

You've already used some of Tableau's modeling features without realizing it. These are the building blocks that today's techniques expand on.

Constant Line

A fixed reference point — a target, a threshold, or a benchmark. Useful for asking "how does our data compare to this specific value?"

Example: A sales target line at $50K per quarter.

Average Line

Shows the mean of your data. Instantly reveals which data points fall above or below the typical value — the simplest form of pattern detection.

Example: Average episode rating across all 10 seasons.

Confidence Intervals

Also known as run charts. Show the expected range around a trend line — if a data point falls outside the band, something unusual may be happening.

Example: Predicting next month's sales within a likely range.

The Connection

These built-in tools answer simple questions: What's typical? What stands out? Today's modeling techniques take the next step — they ask why something stands out and what we can predict from it.

Tool 01

Correlation

Correlation measures how closely two variables move together — and in which direction. Do they rise and fall together, or does one go up while the other goes down?

Strong Negative   -1 No Relationship   0 +1   Strong Positive

Strong Negative

More hours of Netflix → lower exam scores. One goes up while the other goes down.

Example: r = -0.74

Weak / No Correlation

Shoe size → exam scores. No meaningful relationship between the two variables.

Example: r = +0.03

Strong Positive

More hours studying → higher exam scores. Both variables move in the same direction.

Example: r = +0.82

Strength Guide

±0.9 – 1.0   Very strong   |   ±0.7 – 0.9   Strong   |   ±0.4 – 0.7   Moderate   |   ±0.1 – 0.4   Weak   |   0 – 0.1   Little to none

07
Tool 01 — Watch Out

Correlation Is Not Causation

Two variables can move together perfectly and still have nothing to do with each other.

Ice cream sales go up.
Drownings go up.
Does ice cream cause drownings?

The hidden variable: summer.

Hot weather drives both ice cream purchases and swimming. The variable driving both outcomes is called a confounding variable. Controlling for it makes the fake relationship disappear.

Spot the Confounding Variable

Countries with more TV sets per household have longer life expectancy. Does buying a TV make you live longer?

More Examples Worth Knowing

Shoe size & reading ability in children correlate strongly — because age drives both. Older kids have bigger feet and read better.

Hospital quality & patient mortality can correlate negatively — because sicker patients go to better hospitals, not because good hospitals are dangerous.

US per capita mozzarella cheese consumption & civil engineering doctorates awarded tracked almost perfectly for a decade — a classic spurious correlation. No confounding variable, just random noise that happened to line up.

Tool 02

Linear Regression

If I know X, what can I predict about Y? Linear regression draws the line that gets as close as possible to all the data points at once.

What it answers

If this episode aired in year X, what rating would we predict? If a student studies X hours, what score is likely?

When to use it

Your outcome is a number (a score, a price, a temperature) and you expect a roughly straight-line relationship.

When to skip it

Your outcome is yes/no, true/false, or one of several categories. For those, use logistic regression instead.

In Tableau this is the default trend line model. Also called OLS — ordinary least squares.

Average rating by production year — regression line in red
The line is drawn to minimize the total distance between every dot and the line itself. That's the "least squares" part — it's literally minimizing the squared errors.

Tool 03

Logistic Regression

Some questions have a yes or no answer. Will this customer cancel? Will this admitted student enroll? Will Ross and Rachel get back together? Logistic regression estimates the probability of one of those two outcomes.

The S-Curve Explained

Near the bottom: "almost certainly no." Near the top: "almost certainly yes." The steep middle section is where the model is making its toughest judgment calls.

Real-world uses

Credit risk scoring, spam filters, medical diagnosis tools, college admissions yield models, customer churn prediction.

Tool 04

Cluster Analysis

Clustering asks: who belongs together? Without being told the answer in advance, the algorithm finds natural groups in the data based on similarity alone.

The intuition

You don't define the groups beforehand. You give the algorithm the data and it discovers the groupings that make the most internal sense — like sorting a room of strangers into friend groups based on their interests.

How K-means works

You pick a number of groups (k). The algorithm places a center point in each group, assigns every data point to the nearest center, and keeps adjusting until the groups are as tight as possible.

Beyond Today

Where the Field Goes Next

The four tools you learned today are the foundation. Here's a glimpse at what's built on top of them — and what powers the algorithms we'll be critically examining next week.

Decision Trees

Maps out every possible path a decision could take — like a flowchart. Answers questions like "should we approve this loan?" by splitting data on yes/no conditions until it reaches an outcome.

Random Forest

Builds dozens of decision trees and combines their results into one prediction. More accurate than a single tree because it averages out individual errors.

Naive Bayes Classification

Uses probability to sort items into categories based on prior patterns. The backbone of spam filters — it learned what spam looks like from millions of examples.

Neural Networks

Loosely modeled on how the brain processes information. Layers of interconnected nodes learn patterns from massive datasets — the foundation of image recognition, voice assistants, and generative AI.

These are the techniques behind the recommendation engines, content feeds, and matching algorithms we'll explore in this week's case study and readings.
